# How to Choose an AI Keynote Speaker for Your Next Event

Booking a keynote speaker on artificial intelligence used to be a nice-to-have. In 2026, it's a category your audience expects you to get right. The wrong pick — too academic, too hype-driven, or too disconnected from your industry — can quietly tank an otherwise excellent event. Here's how to choose well.

## Start with the question your audience is actually asking

Most event planners default to "we want someone who explains AI." That's a category, not a brief. Push further: are your attendees executives trying to set strategy, practitioners trying to build, or skeptics trying to figure out what's real? Each calls for a different speaker profile.

Once you know the question, you can evaluate speakers against it. A researcher at a frontier lab is great for "where is this technology going?" but often less useful for "what should I do Monday morning?"

## The five questions to ask every candidate

When you have a shortlist, run each speaker through these:

1. What's a recent example of advice you gave that turned out to be wrong, and what did you learn?
2. Walk me through how you'd open a talk for our specific audience.
3. What's the one thing you'd push back on if our CEO said it on stage?
4. How do you handle questions about job displacement, regulation, or risk?
5. What's a topic you actively avoid, and why?

The answers to question one tell you whether the speaker has actually been in the arena. The rest tell you whether they'll meet your audience where they are, or talk past them.

## Watch out for the demo-heavy pitch

Speakers who lean entirely on live demos can be electric in the room — and forgettable a week later. The best AI keynotes leave the audience with a frame for thinking about the technology, not just a list of products they should try. **Insist on a clear thesis** in the speaker's outline before booking.

## Final check: the "boring panel" test

If your speaker would be the most interesting person on a boring panel about your industry — meaning they could connect AI back to *your* operational reality, not just the technology in the abstract — they're a fit. If they'd only be interesting at an AI conference, keep looking.
